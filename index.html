Uttarakhand: Hazards, Vulnerability & Local Experiences

Uttarakhand's mountainous terrain and fragile ecology make it highly disaster-prone, with frequent flash floods, cloudbursts, landslides, and forest fires. Over 50% of Uttarakhand's area is classified as having high or very high landslide susceptibility.

Community Impact & Response

The human toll of intensifying hazards is mounting. During the 2025 monsoon, extreme rains triggered 51 flash floods, 45 major landslides, and 28 cloudburst events across Uttarakhand and Himachal, claiming hundreds of lives.

Raini Village Case Study

‚ñº

Raini village in Chamoli ‚Äì birthplace of the Chipko movement ‚Äì was devastated by the 2021 GLOF/landslide. The community's experience illustrates both vulnerability and resilience:

"It felt like the end of the world."

- Bimla Rana, who watched a flash flood sweep away her mother-in-law and fields
  • Women struggled to secure food, water, and livestock care while coping with psychological stress
  • The village was declared uninhabitable due to destabilized slopes
  • Many residents reluctant to leave ancestral land
  • Gender dynamics: women excluded from relocation decisions

Residents uniformly agreed that "anthropogenic climate change and lopsided development without proper participatory planning" have magnified disaster frequency.

Agricultural Transformation

Traditional crops like wheat and barley are being replaced by apples as farmers attempt to adapt to changing conditions. In the Tons Valley of western Uttarakhand, elders have taken loans and training to establish apple orchards on once-snowy terraced fields.

‚ÑπÔ∏è
Adaptation in Practice: "This year it hailed in March and half the apple blossoms fell," a villager noted, questioning the profitability under volatile weather. This shift brings new uncertainties despite being an adaptation attempt.

Apple Belt Migration

‚ñº

Historical trends include movement of apple orchards to higher altitudes, loss of various tree species, and drying of traditional water sources. Apple cultivation ‚Äì the backbone of Himachal's horticultural economy ‚Äì has shifted upslope as lower elevations grow too warm.

  • Mean minimum temperatures have risen, reducing chilling hours needed for apple fruiting
  • Snowlines have receded, forcing the apple belt to climb upward each year
  • In lower valleys, apples being replaced by vegetables or other fruits
  • More frequent pest attacks and weather-related crop losses

Sikkim: Hazards, Vulnerability & Unique Adaptive Context

Sikkim, in the Eastern Himalaya, is India's least populous state but faces outsized climate risks due to its geography: towering peaks with dozens of glaciers, and deep river gorges prone to flash floods.

October 2023 GLOF Disaster

‚ñº
üö®
Catastrophic Event: The collapse of a morainic dam at South Lhonak Lake unleashed ~50 million cubic meters of water downstream.
  • 20-meter high tsunami-like wave at midnight
  • Estimated as a 1-in-200 year event
  • 55+ deaths, destroyed villages
  • 13 bridges and Teesta Stage-III dam destroyed
  • Lake had expanded 12-fold since 1975 due to glacier melt
  • $500+ million in infrastructure damage
  • ~88,000 people affected (15% of state population)

Scientists pinpoint multiple factors: thawing permafrost and rock fell into the lake, indicating how warming temperatures destabilized the terrain.

Spring Water Crisis

‚ñº

A critical but less dramatic crisis in Sikkim is the drying of mountain springs:

  • Springs are primary water source for 80%+ of rural households
  • 75.9% of households face water scarcity at least part of the year
  • Critical period: January-May dry season
  • Climate drivers: reduced winter precipitation, rainfall shift to fewer, heavier events
  • Women trek longer distances to fetch water

Sikkim's Proactive Measures

‚úÖ
Success Story - Dhara Vikas Program:
  • Launched in 2008 to revive drying springs
  • 50+ dried springs revitalized
  • Nature-based solutions: percolation ponds, check dams, reforestation
  • Community employment through MGNREGA
  • Successfully enhanced water access and economic well-being

Sikkim was the first Indian state to achieve 100% organic farming statewide and formulate a State Action Plan on Climate Change. These efforts build long-term resilience through improved soil moisture retention and reduced landslide-causing runoff.

Challenging Mountain Geography

The landscape itself is a fundamental structural driver of vulnerability:

  • 93% of Uttarakhand's area is hills
  • Steep slopes, high seismicity, fragile soils
  • Proximity to glacial terrain
  • Narrow valleys with little buffer from floods
‚ö†Ô∏è
Barrier: Terrain limits road networks, hampers access to remote communities, and isolates villages during extreme events. After the 2023 monsoon, dozens of hamlets in Himachal were cut off for weeks.
‚úÖ
Enabler: Targeted upgrades (landslide-resistant road designs, rope bridges, robust communication networks) can greatly reduce vulnerability by maintaining access during crises.

Inadequate and Strained Infrastructure

Many villages lack robust basic services:

  • 70%+ of agriculture is rain-fed in Himachal
  • Limited paved roads and bridges
  • Unreliable electricity supply
  • Rural housing often semi-permanent
  • Insufficient healthcare centers

When extreme weather hits, weak infrastructure leads to higher damages and slower recovery. Limited options force maladaptive practices.

Fragile Ecosystems and Environmental Degradation

The Himalayan ecosystem, while rich, is sensitive to disturbances:

  • Deforestation reduces natural defenses
  • Mining and quarrying destabilize slopes
  • Unplanned road cutting increases landslide risk
  • River siltation from erosion and dam projects
  • Loss of forest resources (fodder, medicinal plants)

Environmental mismanagement amplifies hazard intensity through quicker runoff, flashier floods, and "manufactured" disasters.

Attitudinal Drivers & Barriers

Risk Perception and Traditional Beliefs

‚ñº

How communities perceive climate risks influences their willingness to adapt:

"Mountains bring their own weather, we can only endure it."

- Elder in Uttarakhand
‚ö†Ô∏è
Barrier: Fatalistic attitudes can breed complacency. If villagers believe nothing can be done, they may not invest in protective measures.
‚úÖ
Enabler: Growing awareness through lived experience is shifting perceptions. Younger generations and women often have acute awareness that the climate is changing.

Short-term vs Long-term Thinking

‚ñº

Politicians focus on projects yielding visible results within election cycles. Communities struggling with daily needs prioritize immediate gains over future-proofing.

The Supreme Court explicitly called out "governments' indifference towards climate change" as a factor worsening disasters.

After repeated flash floods, parts of Kullu district formed village disaster committees that plan ahead - indicating an attitudinal shift from reactive to proactive.

Social and Cultural Norms

‚ñº

Traditional norms may limit voice of women, lower castes, or youth in community decisions:

"Decisions on mobility (relocation) are primarily made by male heads of the household and community."

- Study on Raini village after 2021 flood

Where communities have embraced more inclusive norms, adaptation outcomes improve. Women's self-help groups engaging in forest fire management show positive change.

Stakeholder Analysis: Government

State Governments and Agencies

‚ñº

Influence: High - formulate State Action Plans on Climate Change, disaster management plans, and development policies.

Culture Code: Balancing development and conservation. Political narrative focuses on bringing roads, industry, and jobs to curb out-migration.

Alignment with SDAS: Varied. Different departments may act at cross-purposes. Post-disaster, governments become more aligned with adaptation, but momentum can fade.

Local Governments (Panchayats)

‚ñº

Influence: Significant at community scale but limited funds and technical know-how.

Culture Code: Community leadership and local politics. Deep understanding of local issues and social dynamics.

Some panchayats have passed resolutions against high-risk projects. When involved in climate planning with adequate resources, they ensure plans are grounded in reality.

Stakeholder Analysis: Communities

Rural Farming Communities

‚ñº

Influence: Collectively significant - manage large swathes of land through daily practices.

Culture Code: Deeply tied to land, tradition, and subsistence. Spiritual/emotional attachment to surroundings.

"Land is what protects us... ensures we are not beggars on a city platform."

- Farmer defending staying in vulnerable village

When adaptation interventions resonate with their needs and knowledge, communities align strongly. Success seen in joint forest management committees.

Women and Self-Help Groups

‚ñº

Influence: Critical for household and community resilience, though often behind-the-scenes.

Culture Code: Resilience, resourcefulness, and community spirit - exemplified by Chipko movement.

  • Women manage water, food, fuel - key to resilience
  • Form SHGs for savings, handicrafts, cooperation
  • Often organize to repair village paths or share water
  • Lead forest fire prevention in many areas

When women's groups are involved in adaptation projects, those projects tend to succeed due to local organization and knowledge.

Indigenous and Tribal Communities

‚ñº

Groups like Lepchas (Sikkim), Bhotiyas (Uttarakhand), tribal areas of Kinnaur/Lahaul-Spiti (HP) have distinct cultures and knowledge systems.

Culture Code: Living in harmony with nature. Lepchas consider themselves guardians of Mt. Khangchendzonga.

Naturally inclined towards sustainable practices. Misalignment happens when outside interventions ignore their autonomy or cultural methods.

Example: Dzumsa traditional councils in North Sikkim now involved in climate planning, respecting customary institutions.

Learning from Nepal

‚úÖ
Key Achievement: Nepal has been a pioneer in community-based adaptation and governance reforms that empower local action.

Community Forestry & Hariyo Ban Program

‚ñº

Nepal's community forestry program (since late 1970s) turned over forest management rights to local user groups:

Hariyo Ban ("Green Forest") Program:

  • Funded by USAID, implemented by WWF, CARE and partners
  • Linked biodiversity conservation, climate adaptation, and livelihoods
  • Activities: slope stabilization, catchment restoration, climate-resilient incomes
  • Community Learning and Action Centers (CLACs) empowered women and marginalized groups

Lesson: Integrating adaptation into community-led natural resource management is highly effective. Large-scale outcomes achievable through federating local efforts.

Local Adaptation Plans of Action (LAPA)

‚ñº

Nepal formalized local adaptation planning starting around 2011:

  • 100+ LAPAs prepared in climate hotspots
  • Village-level participatory vulnerability assessment
  • Community-prioritized actions integrated into development plans
  • Funds spent on locally relevant needs (irrigation ponds, footbridge reinforcement, livelihood training)

Lesson: Decentralized planning with dedicated adaptation focus ensures no village is left behind. Indian states could adopt similar framework.

Learning from Bhutan

Proactive GLOF Risk Reduction

Lesson: Don't wait for catastrophe; invest in preventive measures. Uttarakhand and Sikkim can replicate this approach with their dangerous glacial lakes.

Gross National Happiness & Environmental Protection

‚ñº

Bhutan's development philosophy emphasizes environmental conservation:

  • Constitutional mandate: 60% forest cover forever
  • Currently ~71% forested
  • Carbon-negative country
  • Every proposal assessed for environmental impact
  • Climate adaptation integrated into national planning

Lesson: Strong value-driven framework aligns policies across sectors toward sustainability.

Indigenous Climate Knowledge & Agriculture

‚ñº

Andean indigenous farmers maintain agrobiodiversity:

  • Hundreds of potato varieties adapted to different micro-climates
  • Moving crops to higher altitudes with climate change
  • Variety swapping through networks like Potato Park
  • Traditional weather forecasting combined with meteorological data
  • Crop rotation and mixed cropping for resilience

Lesson: Agrobiodiversity and traditional agroecological practices are key to climate resilience. Re-valuing traditional crops can provide livelihood and resilience gains.

Key Adaptation Capabilities (SDAS)

Seven essential capabilities for Sustainable Development & Adaptation Strategy:

1. Risk Knowledge and Climate Information

‚ñº

The ability to assess, understand, and anticipate climate risks at all levels.

Evidence: States have begun vulnerability assessments. Uttarakhand's climate cell produced district-level vulnerability maps. However, gaps remain - Sikkim's 2023 flood emphasized need for robust monitoring.

Strengthening this capability:

  • Invest in early warning systems (like Bhutan's GLOF alarms)
  • Develop local climate services
  • Ensure communities know risks and can demand action
  • Install Doppler radars and weather stations

2. Infrastructure & Technology Resilience

‚ñº

Capacity to design, build, and maintain infrastructure that withstands climate extremes.

Current gaps: World Bank noted much critical infrastructure in Uttarakhand is not climate-resilient.

Improvements needed:

  • Climate-proof roads, power lines, homes
  • Flood-safe bridges and raised embankments
  • Rainwater harvesting infrastructure
  • Leverage ICT for warning dissemination

3. Institutional Coordination & Governance

‚ñº

Capability to coordinate horizontally (across sectors) and vertically (village to state).

Success example: Kullu, HP pilot brought departments together for risk assessment.

Requirements:

  • Clear roles and communication channels
  • Multi-stakeholder platforms
  • Climate risk task forces
  • Mainstreaming climate across all departments

4. Community Engagement and Social Capital

‚ñº

Strength of community networks, local leadership, and inclusive participation.

Success stories: Nepal's CFUGs and LAPAs achieved large-scale resilience. Quechua communities built water infrastructure themselves.

Building this capability:

  • Train local volunteers (firefighting, first aid)
  • Strengthen customary institutions
  • Ensure gender and caste inclusion
  • Build village disaster committees

5. Livelihood & Financial Adaptiveness

‚ñº

Flexibility of livelihoods and availability of financial tools for adaptation.

Current situation: Communities with diversified livelihoods cope better. Mono-economies suffer when single crops fail.

Development needs:

  • Climate-resilient livelihoods (beekeeping, medicinal plants)
  • Savings groups and SHGs as safety nets
  • Weather-index insurance
  • Microcredit access
  • Economic diversification

6. Ecosystem Management & Nature-based Solutions

‚ñº

Using ecosystems to mitigate climate impacts and sustain resources.

Evidence: Nepal and Peru show restoring ecosystems yields resilience. Sikkim's Dhara Vikas maintained dry-season water.

Actions:

  • Forest departments working with communities
  • Conservation agriculture practices
  • Protecting critical ecosystems
  • Watershed management
  • Traditional knowledge integration

7. Learning and Adaptive Management

‚ñº

Capacity to learn from experiences and adjust strategies accordingly.

Requirements:

  • Monitoring and evaluation systems
  • Annual community review meetings
  • Flexible program funding
  • Documentation of lessons learned
  • Culture of iterative improvement

Seven Major Blockers to Adaptation

‚ö†Ô∏è
Critical Challenge: These seven blockers must be addressed for effective adaptation in the Himalayan states.

1. Governance Silos and Weak Coordination

‚ñº

Problem: Agencies don't share information or plan together, efforts are fragmented.

Evidence: Uttarakhand's response agencies lacking coordination framework. Forest, agriculture, water sectors operate in isolation.

Solution: Set up integrated task forces like Nepal's Climate Change Council to force coordination.

2. Inadequate Funding and Economic Constraints

‚ñº

Problem: Insufficient or ill-targeted funding for adaptation.

Evidence: Mountain areas get less per-capita investment despite higher unit costs. Post-disaster relief dwarfs pre-disaster investment.

Solution: Innovative financing - earmark development budget percentage for adaptation, use insurance, engage private sector.

3. Social Inequity and Exclusion

‚ñº

Problem: Women, lower castes, tribes, and poor are excluded.

Evidence: Women excluded from decision-making in Raini. SCs excluded from land and governance.

Solution: Strong social inclusion mandates, monitoring, and empowerment (women-led funds, etc.).

4. Short-term Mindset and Political Indifference

‚ñº

Problem: Focus on immediate gains vs. long-term resilience.

Evidence: Supreme Court calling out "governments' indifference towards climate change."

Solution: Public pressure, political champions, legislation requiring climate risk disclosure.

5. Limited Awareness and Capacity at Local Level

‚ñº

Problem: Communities and officials may not understand climate projections or options.

Evidence: Fatalistic attitudes, technical capacity gaps in climate-resilient design.

Solution: Education campaigns, training "climate ambassadors," school-based programs.

6. Environmental Degradation and Competing Land Use

‚ñº

Problem: Poor environmental management undermines adaptation.

Evidence: Deforestation and forest fires increase vulnerability. Mining and mass tourism cause degradation.

Solution: Enforce environmental laws, provide alternatives (green jobs vs. mining), sustainable development.

7. Emergency Response Bias and Lack of Adaptive Learning

‚ñº

Problem: Focus on disaster relief rather than risk reduction.

Evidence: After 2013 floods, relocation plans made but implementation lagged once memory faded.

Solution: Shift budgeting to prevention, institutionalize learning through mandatory after-action reviews.

Inclusion and Missing Voices

‚ÑπÔ∏è
Key Principle: While climate change affects everyone, not everyone's voice is heard equally in crafting solutions.

Women and Gender Perspectives

‚ñº

Women face a "double burden" - disproportionately responsible for water, fuel, and caregiving, yet historically excluded from decision-making.

"It was hard to provide family members with food and clean water‚Ä¶we felt extreme stress and trauma."

- Women of Raini after 2021 disaster

Impacts on Women:

  • Feminization of agriculture due to male out-migration
  • Increased burden from drying springs and erratic rain
  • Excluded from relocation decisions
  • Physical strain from longer walks for water/fuel
  • Less time for healthcare and rest

Inclusion Strategies:

  • Quotas for women in committees (33% in panchayats)
  • Women facilitators and extension workers
  • Meeting schedules convenient for women
  • Childcare during meetings
  • Targeted livelihood programs

Scheduled Castes and Marginalized Groups

‚ñº

Scheduled Castes often have less land (usually marginal lands) and poorer housing, making them more vulnerable.

Evidence from Uttarakhand: SC family owned only 5% of village land despite being 20% of population, mostly in hazard-prone areas.

Inclusion Strategies:

  • Social vulnerability mapping at village level
  • Separate focus group discussions
  • Infrastructure protection for SC hamlets
  • Land swaps or secure housing in safer locations
  • Social inclusion index for funding prioritization

Indigenous and Tribal Communities

‚ñº

Groups like Lepchas, Bhutias, Scheduled Tribes have distinct identities and deep environmental knowledge.

Impacts:

  • Live in environmentally fragile but rich areas
  • Climate threatens resource base
  • Spiritual loss from melting sacred glaciers
  • Fewer alternative livelihoods

Inclusion Strategies:

  • Respect self-determination and knowledge systems
  • Use customary institutions (like Dzumsa)
  • Provide information in local languages
  • Employ local youth as project staff
  • Strengthen land tenure rights

The Poor, Disabled, and Elderly

‚ñº

The Poor and Landless:

  • Live in most precarious locations
  • No savings, insurance, or social connections
  • Often invisible in damage assessments
  • May resort to negative coping strategies

Disabled Individuals:

  • Unique evacuation needs
  • Accessible shelter requirements
  • Assistive device replacement after disasters
  • Ensuring warnings reach visually/hearing impaired

Elderly:

  • Hold valuable traditional knowledge
  • May prioritize different adaptation measures
  • Physical limitations for heavy adaptation work
  • Memory of past climate anomalies valuable for mapping

Immediate Actions (0-2 years)

Long-term Vision (5-10 years)

  • Economic Transformation: Green economy transition
  • Land Use Planning: Climate-informed zoning, managed retreat where needed
  • Regional Cooperation: Transboundary risk management
  • Knowledge Systems: Traditional-modern knowledge integration
  • Sustainable Tourism: Eco-tourism replacing mass tourism

Transformative Changes

  • Governance Reform: Climate mainstreaming across all sectors
  • Social Justice: Inclusive development ensuring no one left behind
